Freelance writing in 2026 looks very different from even two years ago. Clients expect faster
turnaround, editors expect cleaner first drafts, and the writers keeping up are the ones using AI
tools strategically — not to replace their voice, but to speed up research, editing, and the
unglamorous parts of the job.

Here's what's actually worth using.

## For first drafts and brainstorming

**[ChatGPT](/tools/chatgpt/)** remains the most versatile starting point — it's good at
outlining, working through structure problems, and generating angles you hadn't considered. The
free tier is genuinely usable for most freelance workloads.

**[Claude](/tools/claude/)** is worth having alongside ChatGPT specifically for longer pieces —
its large context window means you can paste in a full brief, style guide, and past examples of
your writing in one go, and it holds onto all of it.

## For polishing and tone

**[Grammarly](/tools/grammarly/)** is still the baseline every freelancer should have running in
the background — not for grammar alone, but for catching tone drift across a long draft.

## For research

**[Perplexity](/tools/perplexity/)** has become the go-to replacement for manual Googling when
you need cited, current information fast — especially useful when a client wants sources you can
point to.

## The bottom line

None of these tools write the piece for you — clients can tell, and editors increasingly can too.
The writers getting the most value are using AI for the 30% of the job that isn't actually
writing: outlining, fact-checking, and tightening prose after the first draft is done.
